ON THE CONE CONJECTURE FOR LOG CALABI-YAU MIRRORS OF
FANO 3-FOLDS

JENNIFER LI


Abstract. Let Y be a smooth projective 3-fold admitting a K3 fibration f : Y → P[1] with
−KY = f [∗]O(1). We show that the pseudoautomorphism group of Y acts with finitely many orbits
on the codimension one faces of the movable cone if H [3](Y, C) = 0, confirming a special case of
the Kawamata–Morrison–Totaro cone conjecture. In [CCGK16] [P18], and [CP18], the authors
construct log Calabi-Yau 3-folds with K3 fibrations satisfying the hypotheses of our theorem as
the mirrors of Fano 3-folds.

1. Introduction


Morrison’s cone conjecture is stated as follows:

Conjecture 1.1. (The Morrison cone conjecture) Let X be a Calabi-Yau 3-fold. Then,
(1) The automorphism group of X acts on the nef cone of X with a rational polyhedral fundamental domain; and
(2) The pseudoautomorphism group of X (see Definition 1.7 below) acts on the movable cone
of X with a rational polyhedral fundamental domain.

In particular, Morrison’s conjecture implies the following:
(1) The automorphism group of X acts on the faces of the nef cone of X with finitely many
orbits; and
(2) The pseudoautomorphism group of X acts on the faces of the movable cone of X with
finitely many orbits.
The Morrison cone conjecture was generalized by Totaro to klt pairs (see [T10]). In this paper,
we prove a version of the cone conjecture for certain types of log Calabi-Yau 3-folds, namely, for
smooth projective 3-folds Y that admit a K3 fibration f : Y → P[1] such that −KY = f [∗]O(1).

Remark 1.2. If f : Y → P[1] is a K3 fibration such that KY is relatively nef, −KY is effective and
nonzero, f is not isotrivial, and f has no multiple fibers, then −KY = f [∗]O(1). This follows from
positivity of the pushforward of the relative canonical bundle, see e.g. [F78].

Remark 1.3. If D is any smooth (or reduced normal crossing) fiber of f, then (Y, D) is log CalabiYau.


Definition 1.4. The cone of curves Curv(Y ) is defined as:

Curv(Y ) = {[�] ai[Ci] | ai ∈ R≥0 and Ci ⊂ Y is a curve }.

Definition 1.5. The nef cone Nef(Y ) is the dual cone of the cone of curves. The nef effective
cone is defined as Nef [e](Y ) := Nef(Y ) ∩ Eff(Y ).


Definition 1.6. The movable cone is defined as:
Mov(Y ) := ⟨B | B is a divisor with |B| having no fixed part⟩R≥0.

The movable effective cone is defined as Mov [e](Y ) := Mov(Y ) ∩ Eff(Y ).

Definition 1.7. A pseudoautomorphism of a variety Y is a birational map θ : Y ��� Y that is an
isomorphism outside of codimension 2 subsets of the domain and codomain.

Definition 1.8. A K3 fibration is a morphism f : Y → B such that the general fiber F ⊂ Y of f
is a K3 surface.

Here we briefly lay out some key results used in this paper. First of all, we note that our setup is
a special case of the Kawamata-Morrison-Totaro cone conjecture (see Theorem 2.1). This allows
us to conclude that Mov(Y ) is the union of the nef cones of all flops of Y (see Theorem 2.2).
Two important results that we will use next are Mori’s cone theorem ([KM98], Theorem 1.24) and
Mori’s classification of extremal rays of the cone of curves for smooth 3-folds ([M82]). Given a
smooth projective variety Y, Mori’s cone theorem describes the cone of curves of Y in the region
KY < 0. Mori gives a classification of the contractions contR : Y → X associated to extremal
rays R ⊂ Curv(Y ) in the region KY < 0 when Y is a smooth projective 3-fold over C. There are
a total of eight possibilities (see [M82] or [KM98]).
Now suppose that Y is a smooth projective 3-fold and f : Y → P[1] is a K3 fibration such that
−KY = f [∗]O(1). In this setting, we show (Proposition 2.5) that for any Z obtained by a sequence
of flops of Y, six of the eight possible types of extremal rays of Curv(Z) in the region KZ < 0 can
be eliminated, leaving two remaining types:
(1) Type (1): the blowup of a smooth curve Γ; or
(2) Type (6): a conic bundle.

We prove the following main results.

Theorem 1.9. Let Y be a smooth projective 3-fold and f : Y → P[1] a K3 fibration such that
−KY = f [∗]O(1). Then the pseudoautomorphism group PsAut(Y ) of Y acts on
(1) the Type (6) faces of Mov [e](Y ) with finitely many orbits;
(2) the Type (1) faces of Mov [e](Y ) with finitely many orbits if H [3](Y, C) = 0.

Remark 1.10. If H [3](Y, C) = 0, then any contraction of Type (1) has genus g = 0.

By a result of Kawamata, we can also take care of all curves on K [⊥]:

Theorem 1.11. (Kawamata [K97]) PsAut(Y ) acts on the faces of Mov [e](Y ) containing −KY
with finitely many orbits.

Remark 1.12. Because −KY (a fiber) is nef, there are no curves in the region K - 0.

Theorem 1.13. PsAut(Y ) acts on the codimension 1 faces of Mov [e](Y ) with finitely many orbits
if H [3](Y, C) = 0.

Conjecture 1.14. PsAut(Y ) acts with finitely many orbits on the faces of Mov [e](Y ) of Type (1)
for any genus g ≥ 0.

Theorem 1.15. Assume that Conjecture 1.14 holds. Then PsAut(Y ) acts with finitely many
orbits on the codimension 1 faces of Mov [e](Y ).

In Section 3, we show that there are many examples of smooth projective 3-folds Y admitting a
K3 fibration f : Y → P[1] such that −KY = f [∗]O(1) and Y has infinite pseudoautomorphism group.

3. Examples

Let S be a K3 surface over a field K of characteristic zero. Assume that K is the function
field of a complex curve. Then the Brauer group of K is trivial (Tsen’s theorem). Then, writing
G = Gal( K/K[¯] ) for the absolute Galois group of K, Pic(S) = Pic(S ⊗K K[¯] )[G], see [H16], Chapter
17 §2.2. Assume in addition that G acts trivially on Pic(S ⊗K K[¯] ), so that Pic(S) = Pic(S ⊗K K[¯] ).
Let W ⊂ Aut(Pic(S)) be the Weyl group generated by the reflections
sα(x) = x + (x.α)α
for α ∈ Pic(S) such that α[2] = −2. Then Aut(S) is commensurable with Aut(Pic(S))/W . This
follows from the Torelli theorem over K (Theorem 2.6), cf. [H16], Chapter 15 Corollary 2.7. In
particular:

Proposition 3.1. With notation as above, Aut(S) is finite if and only if W ⊂ Aut(Pic(S)) has
finite index. Then the lattice Pic(S) is called 2-reflective in [D83]. 
Remark 3.2. If G does not act trivially on Pic(S ⊗K K[¯] ), then one can define a reflection group
W [′] such that Aut(S) is commensurable with Aut(Pic(S))/W [′] (cf. [OS01], Definition 1.6), but
W [′] ⊈ W in general.

Many examples are obtained via mirror symmetry for Fano 3-folds. Let X be a Fano 3-fold and
E ⊂ X a smooth divisor such that KX +E = 0. Then there exists an associated smooth projective
3-fold Y and a K3-fibration f : Y → P[1] such that −KY = f [∗]O(1) and H [3](Y, Z) = 0 and there is a
fiber D of f which is a normal crossing divisor with a 0-dimensional stratum ([CCGK16], [CP18],

[P18] [DHKOP23]). In particular, f : Y → P[1] satisfies the hypotheses of Theorem 1.13.

Remark 3.3. The Fano 3-fold X is mirror to the K3-fibration f : Y \ D → A[1] (a so-called LandauGinzburg model).

Moreover, the Picard lattice Pic(Yη¯) of the geometric generic fiber Yη¯ = Yη ⊗C(P1) C(P[1]) of f : Y →
P[1] and the action of the Galois group on it were computed ([P18], [DHKOP23]). There are 105
deformation types of Fano 3-folds. We will use the notation of Mori-Mukai (as in [CCGK16]) for
the Fano 3-folds of Picard rank at least 2. The Galois group action on Pic(Yη¯) is trivial except in
cases 2.12, 4.3, 6.1, 7.1, 8.1, 9.1, and 10.1. In the remaining cases, the lattice Pic(Yη) = Pic(Yη¯) is
not 2-reflective except in cases 2.1, 3.2, 3.6, 4.9, and the Picard rank 1 cases X6 ⊂ P(1, 1, 1, 1, 3)
and X6 ⊂ P(1, 1, 1, 2, 3). Here we use Nikulin’s classification of 2-reflective lattices (cf. [D83],
Theorem 2.2.2). In the remaining 105 − 13 = 92 cases, Aut(Yη) is infinite by Proposition 3.1, so
PsAut(Y ) is infinite (and so Mov(Y ) is not rational polyhedral).
